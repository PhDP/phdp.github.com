Short personal statements on open access in scientific publications and open source software.

Open Access

Science is a collective project and I cannot see how, in the era of the internet, we can justify hiding our (often publicly funded) research output. Scientific papers should be available before being submitted to journals for peer-review using services such as arXiv, github, bitbucket, FigShare , etc... I always submit my first-author manuscripts to one of these services before submitting them to a journal and if the journal does not tolerate it (e.g.: Ecology Letters), then I submit elsewhere. Also, I always either store the final PDF on my website or, in the case of open access publications, link to my paper on the journal's webpage.

I cannot submit the manuscript to arXiv when I'm not the first author, though I certainly hope the first author will do so. However, the final PDF will always be accessible on my website.

Open Source

I'm comfortable with the full range of licenses: from the liberal MIT to the more restrictive GPL and even proprietary software . In practice, I think the basic infrastructure on which we build software has to be open source. It's the only way to ensure that no single corporation is in a position to impose its will on software development. If software is indeed eating the world, we would be crazy to let . Thus, I strongly favor open source operating systems, browsers, databases, compilers, interpreters. When it comes to more specialized software: the more liberal the better (MIT, Apache), copyleft if necessary (it did serve Linux well), but I also understand the need to keep the code private and adopt a proprietary license.

Open Access == Open Source?

As you can see, my positions on open access and open source are rather different.

In short, openess is one way to achieve progress, but other constains might. The rise of Linux was heavily supported by IBM (server-side), and thanks to Android and Chrome OS, Google has made more to put Linux devices in the users' hand than... well, pretty much anyone. It seems to me that, in the current economic system, companies with import proprieary software made more for free software than purists.

There are many arguments against my position. One is that the current economic system is simply wrong. There is certainly some truth to it, though my position reflect what I think is right now, not in some hypothetical system.
